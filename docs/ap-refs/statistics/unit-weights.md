## Unit topic breakdown (what to test inside each unit)

### Unit 1 — Exploring One-Variable Data (15-23%)
Distributions (shape/center/spread), center-and-spread metrics (mean/median/SD/IQR), boxplots, histograms, z-scores, normal distribution and empirical rule.

### Unit 2 — Exploring Two-Variable Data (5-7%)
Scatterplots, correlation coefficient (r), least-squares regression, residual plots, influential points, r² interpretation.

### Unit 3 — Collecting Data (12-15%)
Sampling methods (SRS, stratified, cluster, systematic), observational study vs experiment, randomization, bias (selection/non-response/response), blocking, random assignment.

### Unit 4 — Probability, Random Variables, Probability Distributions (10-20%)
Addition/multiplication rules, conditional probability, independence, discrete RV mean/variance, binomial distribution, geometric distribution, continuous RVs, normal distribution probabilities.

### Unit 5 — Sampling Distributions (7-12%)
Sampling distribution of sample means (CLT), sampling distribution of sample proportions, standard error, conditions for normality.

### Unit 6 — Inference for Proportions (12-15%)
Confidence intervals for 1-prop and 2-prop, hypothesis tests for 1-prop and 2-prop (z-tests), Type I/II errors, power, conditions (10% rule, large counts).

### Unit 7 — Inference for Means (10-18%)
Confidence intervals for 1-mean and 2-means, t-tests (1-sample, 2-sample, paired), matched-pairs design, conditions for t-procedures.

### Unit 8 — Chi-Square (2-5%)
Chi-square goodness-of-fit, chi-square test for independence, chi-square test for homogeneity, expected counts.

### Unit 9 — Inference for Slopes (2-5%)
Regression t-test, confidence interval for slope, conditions for inference on slope.

## Generation strategy

When generating an MCQ for AP Statistics:
1. Pick a unit weighted by exam weight.
2. Pick a course skill (over-sample Skill 3 since it's 30-40% of exam).
3. Ground the question in a concrete scenario (sports, polls, medicine, social sciences).
4. Provide numerical data students must reason over — not just recall definitions.
5. Write 4 distractors hitting common misconceptions (e.g. confusing Type I vs II, misreading p-value as probability the null is true).
