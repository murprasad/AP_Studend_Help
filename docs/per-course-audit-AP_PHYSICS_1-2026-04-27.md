### Sample 1 — cmo3sjzr70 [HARD | PHY1_6_SIMPLE_HARMONIC_MOTION]

**Stimulus** (596 chars):
> Diagram description: Two side-by-side setups on frictionless horizontal surfaces. LEFT SETUP: A wall on the left with a spring (spring constant k, natural length shown) attached horizontally to a block labeled 'm'. An arrow shows displacement 'A' to the right of the equilibrium position (marked with a dashed vertical line). The block is shown at position A with velocity = 0. RIGHT SETUP: Identical spring (same k) attached to a larger block labeled '4m', also displaced the same distance 'A' from equilibrium with velocity = 0. Both springs are shown compressed/stretched by the same amount A.

**Question** (137 chars): A block of mass m and then 4m are attached to a spring, displaced distance A, and released. How do period and max kinetic energy compare?

**Options:**
- A) The period quadruples and the maximum kinetic energy is the same for both experiments.
- B) The period doubles and the maximum kinetic energy is four times greater for the 4m experiment.
- C) The period doubles and the maximum kinetic energy is the same for both experiments.
- D) The period doubles and the maximum kinetic energy is two times greater for the 4m experiment.

**Correct:** C

**Explanation** (297 chars): Correct answer's reasoning is based on the period-mass relationship and kinetic energy equation. The period doubles due to the relationship T_new = 2π√(4m/k) = 2T_original. Maximum kinetic energy remains the same as KE_max = (1/2)kA², which depends only on spring constant and amplitude, not mass.

### Sample 2 — cmo0m0amz0 [MEDIUM | PHY1_6_SIMPLE_HARMONIC_MOTION]

**Stimulus** (172 chars):
> | Given | Value (units) |
> |-------|---------------|
> | Original mass | 0.50 kg |
> | Original period | 2.0 s |
> | New mass | 2.0 kg |
> | Spring constant | Same for both trials |

**Question** (172 chars): A 0.50 kg mass attached to a spring oscillates with a period of 2.0 s. If the mass is replaced with a 2.0 kg mass on the same spring, what is the new period of oscillation?

**Options:**
- A) 1.0 s
- B) 8.0 s
- C) 4.0 s
- D) 2.0 s

**Correct:** C

**Explanation** (443 chars): The period of a mass-spring system is given by T = 2π√(m/k). Since the spring constant k remains constant, the ratio of periods depends on the square root of the mass ratio: T₂/T₁ = √(m₂/m₁). Here, m₂/m₁ = 2.0/0.50 = 4, so √4 = 2. Therefore, T₂ = 2 × 2.0 s = 4.0 s. Distractor A incorrectly inverts the mass ratio; distractor B ignores the mass change entirely; distractor D forgets to take the square root and uses the raw mass ratio instead.

### Sample 3 — cmo2ws5kj0 [MEDIUM | PHY1_2_FORCES_AND_NEWTONS_LAWS]

**Question** (127 chars): A box is pulled up a frictionless incline by a rope. Which of the following forces should be included in the free body diagram?

**Options:**
- A) Normal force, tension, and gravity
- B) Normal force, gravity, and applied force
- C) Normal force, tension, and friction
- D) Tension, friction, and gravity

**Correct:** A

**Explanation** (326 chars): To draw a free body diagram of the box, we must include all the forces acting on it. The normal force acts perpendicular to the incline, tension acts along the rope, and gravity acts downward. Since the incline is frictionless, there is no friction force. The applied force is already accounted for as the tension in the rope.

### Sample 5 — cmo2xonxr0 [MEDIUM | PHY1_5_MOMENTUM]

**Stimulus** (214 chars):
> | Given | Value (units) |
> |-------|---------------|
> | Mass of ball | 0.50 kg |
> | Velocity before collision | 12 m/s (toward wall) |
> | Velocity after collision | 8 m/s (away from wall) |
> | Time of contact | 0.10 s |

**Question** (141 chars): A 0.50 kg ball traveling at 12 m/s strikes a wall and bounces back at 8 m/s. Which statement best explains the impulse delivered by the wall?

**Options:**
- A) The impulse is 10 N·s directed away from the wall because the change in momentum is (0.50)[8 − (−12)] = 10 kg·m/s.
- B) The impulse is 4 N·s directed away from the wall because only the final velocity determines impulse.
- C) The impulse is 20 N·s directed toward the wall because the wall reverses the ball's direction.
- D) The impulse is 10 N·s directed away from the wall because the change in momentum is (0.50)(8 − 12) = −2 kg·m/s.

**Correct:** A

**Explanation** (243 chars): Impulse equals the change in momentum: J = Δp = m(v_f − v_i). Taking the direction away from the wall as positive, v_i = −12 m/s and v_f = +8 m/s. Thus J = 0.50 kg × [8 − (−12)] m/s = 0.50 × 20 = 10 kg·m/s = 10 N·s directed away from the wall.
